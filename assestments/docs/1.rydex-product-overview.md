## Part A — Executive Summary

> [!abstract] Purpose of this document This is the single-page-deep entry point to Rydex for anyone — stakeholder, new engineer, or reviewer — who needs to understand **what Rydex is, why it exists, and how its success will be judged** before going deeper into the [[1-PRD|full PRD]] or the [[3-End-to-End-Delivery-Plan|delivery plan]]. It belongs to **Phase 0 (Discovery & Documentation)** of the delivery plan and feeds the [[2.rydex-requirements-and-risk|traceability matrix]].

## A1. What Rydex Is

Rydex is a real-time ride-hailing and delivery dispatch platform. It connects riders and senders with nearby drivers, matches them intelligently, prices trips dynamically based on live supply and demand, and keeps both sides of the marketplace informed throughout the trip.

> [!note] Working title "Rydex" is a working title. The architecture, scope, and documentation are what matter — naming can change without touching any of the substance below.

Rydex is **not** built as a toy demo. It is built to demonstrate that a small, well-engineered team can design and ship a system with the same architectural rigor as the ride-hailing platforms people use every day — load-tested, monitored, and reasoned about the way production systems are.

### What Rydex deliberately is _not_ (yet)

- ❌ Not a multi-city commercial operation — it's architected to behave like one, but launches as a **single-region demonstration system**
- ❌ Not a payments processor — it **integrates** with a real payment provider rather than building one
- ❌ Not a mapping/routing provider — it **integrates** with existing map and routing data rather than building proprietary maps

This boundary matters: it keeps the team honest about what was actually _built_ versus what was _integrated_, which is the distinction a credible reviewer will look for first.

## A2. The Problem

> [!question] The real-world problem, stated plainly Connecting someone who needs a ride or delivery with someone available to provide it — fast, fairly, and safely — at a moment's notice, anywhere in a city, at any volume of demand.

This is hard for four concrete reasons:

|Why it's hard|In practice|
|---|---|
|**Supply is moving**|The "right" driver for a rider changes every second because drivers are in motion|
|**Demand is spiky**|Rush hour, weather, and events cause sudden imbalance; supply has to respond|
|**Trust isn't optional**|Riders and drivers are strangers meeting through an app — safety and fraud prevention are foundational, not features|
|**Latency is felt instantly**|A 5-second delay in seeing a driver move on the map breaks the experience|

## A3. Vision & Mission

> [!quote] Vision Statement A dispatch platform where every match is fast and fair, every price reflects real conditions, every trip is trackable in real time, and the system itself is observable enough that problems are caught before users notice them.

**Mission (how the vision gets executed):** Build and prove out — end to end, with evidence rather than assumptions — the core marketplace mechanics of a real-time dispatch platform: intelligent matching, transparent dynamic pricing, live two-sided tracking, and AI-assisted trust & support — at a scope and fidelity that would survive a serious technical review.

## A4. Who Rydex Serves

|Persona|Core need|
|---|---|
|**Rider**|Fast matching, accurate ETA, transparent pricing, a sense of safety|
|**Driver**|Fair access to ride offers, predictable earnings, low-friction navigation|
|**Sender** (delivery variant)|Reliable pickup/drop-off, live tracking, proof of delivery|
|**Ops/Support Agent**|Visibility into trips, ability to intervene, fast dispute resolution tools|
|**Platform Admin**|Control over pricing rules, zones, and driver onboarding without needing engineering help|

## A5. Objectives

These are the outcomes Rydex is organized around achieving, derived directly from the PRD's value propositions and non-functional requirements:

1. **Match riders and drivers in seconds, not minutes** — under normal load, with the speed claim backed by load-test evidence, not assumption
2. **Price transparently and responsively** — surge reflects _real_, measured supply/demand imbalance per zone, and explainably unwinds as conditions normalize
3. **Make every trip trackable in real time** — location and status updates feel instantaneous to both rider and driver
4. **Build trust as infrastructure, not an afterthought** — fraud/anomaly detection flags the obvious, costly cases automatically; humans stay in the loop on every decision that matters
5. **Deflect support load with grounded AI** — answer from the platform's own documented policy, escalate honestly when it can't, never guess
6. **Prove the system, don't just claim it** — every scalability, latency, and uptime claim in this document and the PRD is backed by a real load-test report, not an estimate

## A6. Value Proposition Summary

|For Riders|For Drivers|For the Business|
|---|---|---|
|Matched in seconds, not minutes|Fair, transparent ride offers|Efficient marketplace → more completed trips|
|Always know where your driver is|Earnings reflect real demand (surge)|Fraud caught early → lower losses|
|Pricing that makes sense, not arbitrary|Less idle time waiting for rides|System stays up under load → trust|
|Instant help via AI assistant|Instant support via AI assistant|Lower support costs via AI deflection|

## A9. Key Risks (Top-Line)

> [!warning] Full register with owners and mitigations lives in [[2.rydex-requirements-and-risk|risk register]]. Summarized here for visibility at the executive level.

- **Small team, bounded scope** — Rydex is deliberately scoped to a single region/simulation, not multi-region commercial scale
- **Third-party dependency risk** — maps/routing, payments, and AI providers are integrated, not built; their outages or limits are external risk
- **AI is probabilistic, not perfect** — fraud detection and forecasting accuracy will be reported honestly with measured numbers, never assumed flawless
- **Location data is sensitive** — privacy and access control are a day-one requirement, not a later add-on -e

## Part B — Vision & Strategy

> [!abstract] Purpose of this document The [Executive Summary (Part A above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-a--executive-summary) states _what_ Rydex is and _what_ it's judged by. This document explains the _reasoning_: why this approach to building it, what we're deliberately betting on, what we're deliberately not doing, and how the bets are sequenced. If the Executive Summary is the headline, this is the editorial behind it.

## B1. Vision

> [!quote] Vision Statement A dispatch platform where every match is fast and fair, every price reflects real conditions, every trip is trackable in real time, and the system itself is observable enough that problems are caught before users notice them.

This vision has four load-bearing words, and each one rules things out as much as it points somewhere:

|Word|What it commits us to|What it rules out|
|---|---|---|
|**Fast**|Matching measured in seconds, with evidence|"Eventually consistent is good enough" thinking for the match itself|
|**Fair**|Drivers waiting longest get priority; batch matching optimizes globally, not greedily|A system that's fast for riders but extractive toward drivers|
|**Real time**|Sub-second-feeling location/status updates via persistent connections|Polling, "refresh to check," anything that announces its own latency|
|**Observable**|Problems caught on a dashboard before a user complains|Shipping instrumentation as an afterthought once something breaks|

## B2. Strategic Thesis

> [!important] The core bet this project makes A small, disciplined team can build a system with the **architectural rigor** of a production ride-hailing platform — not the feature breadth, not the commercial scale, but the _rigor_: the same categories of decision (consistency tradeoffs, service boundaries, load evidence) made with the same seriousness.

This is a deliberate substitution. Rydex does not compete on breadth (multi-city, multi-modal, payments innovation). It competes on **depth of correctness in a narrow scope**. That trade is the strategy.

### Why this thesis, and not the alternative

The alternative strategy — maximize feature surface area, demo well, skip the "boring" production-craft work — is the more common path for a portfolio-scale system. It was deliberately rejected here because:

- A wide-but-shallow system is easy to discredit in five minutes of technical questioning (_"what happens when Redis goes down?"_)
- A narrow-but-deep system survives that questioning, because every claim is backed by something built, not assumed
- The PRD's own scoping decision (§4) — single-region, integrate-don't-build payments/maps — already encodes this thesis; this strategy document just makes the reasoning explicit

## B3. Strategic Pillars

Four pillars carry the vision into engineering reality. Each maps to a section of the [[1-PRD|PRD]] and a tier of the [[2-Engineering-Domain-Roadmap|Engineering Domain Roadmap]].

### Pillar 1 — Matching is the product

Everything else (pricing, tracking, trust) exists in service of one core exchange: connecting the right rider to the right driver, fast. The matching engine is architected as a swappable, benchmarkable component (PRD Epic D) precisely because it's expected to be replaced and improved over the roadmap, not bolted down early.

> **Strategic implication:** the matching engine interface (`IMatchingStrategy`) is designed _before_ the first strategy is implemented. Optionality here is cheap now and expensive later — this is one of the few places the project explicitly pays a small upfront design cost to avoid a rewrite.

### Pillar 2 — Trust is infrastructure, not a feature

Fraud detection and dispute handling are not Beta polish — they're treated as a non-negotiable layer because the product's entire premise (strangers transacting through an app) collapses without them. The strategic choice here is **scoring, never auto-blocking** (PRD §10, §6 Epic F): the system flags, a human decides. This protects against the single worst failure mode for a trust system — false positives that alienate legitimate users — at the cost of needing a human-in-the-loop workflow.

### Pillar 3 — Honesty about what AI does

The PRD (§10) is unusually explicit that AI features are described by what they _actually are_ (a retrieval-grounded assistant, a triage model) versus what they're _not_ (a general chatbot, a guarantee). This is a strategic choice, not just a writing style: overpromising AI capability is the fastest way to lose credibility with both users and technical reviewers, and the cost of that honesty is paid once, up front, in scoping — not later, in damage control.

### Pillar 4 — Evidence over assertion

Every NFR (PRD §11) is written to be _measured_, not asserted. "Scalability" is defined as "tested to handle a defined number of concurrent trips, with the actual tested number reported." This is the single most repeated strategic pattern across the entire document set — it shows up again in the [[3-End-to-End-Delivery-Plan|Delivery Plan]]'s Phase 7 (Evidence Pass) and in the [Executive Summary (Part A above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-a--executive-summary)'s Objective 6.

## B4. What We Are Optimizing For (and What We're Trading Away)

Strategy is mostly the discipline of saying no. These are the explicit trades:

|We optimize for|We accept the cost of|
|---|---|
|Architectural depth in a narrow scope|Not having multi-city/multi-modal breadth|
|Evidence-backed claims (load tests, measured precision)|Slower path to "feature complete" — testing and observability are built alongside features, not after|
|Integrating payments/maps rather than building them|Being dependent on third-party reliability and limits (tracked in [[Rydex-Requirements-and-Risk|
|Human-in-the-loop trust & safety|Slower resolution on flagged trips than a fully automated (and less trustworthy) system|
|Designing the matching engine interface before the first strategy exists|A small amount of upfront design time that doesn't ship a visible feature|

## B5. Sequencing Logic — Why the Roadmap Is Ordered This Way

The roadmap (detailed in [Feature Roadmap (Part E above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-e--feature-roadmap) and the [[3-End-to-End-Delivery-Plan|Delivery Plan]]) isn't an arbitrary list of milestones — each one exists to _prove_ a specific claim before the next one is attempted:

1. **Alpha proves the core loop exists** — before investing in real-time infrastructure, confirm a trip can be requested, matched, and recorded at all
2. **Beta 1 proves it feels real-time** — before making matching "smart," confirm the live-update experience actually holds up, since smarter matching on a laggy UI wouldn't be felt
3. **Beta 2 proves the marketplace is intelligent** — batch matching and surge pricing only matter once there's a real-time substrate to display their effects on
4. **Beta 3 proves trust & support are credible** — deliberately sequenced _after_ the marketplace works, because fraud detection needs real trip data patterns to be meaningful, not synthetic ones
5. **Release Candidate proves the claims are true** — load testing is the last gate specifically because it's measuring a system that's now feature-complete enough for the numbers to mean something
6. **Showcase Release proves it's communicable** — a correct system that can't be clearly explained to a reviewer hasn't finished the job

> [!note] The sequencing rule Each milestone produces an artifact (working code, a measured number, a documented decision) that the _next_ milestone depends on. This mirrors the dependency logic already used in the [[3-End-to-End-Delivery-Plan|Delivery Plan]]'s phase structure — strategy and delivery sequencing are the same logic applied at two different altitudes.

## B6. Strategic Risks to the Thesis Itself

These are distinct from the tactical risks in [[2.rydex-requirements-and-risk|risk register]] — they're risks to the _strategy_, not the system:

- **Scope creep toward breadth** — the constant temptation to add "just one more" feature that looks impressive in a demo but dilutes the depth-over-breadth bet
- **Evidence theater** — producing dashboards and load-test reports that exist but don't actually get looked at or acted on, which would quietly convert Pillar 4 from substance back into assertion
- **AI honesty erosion under pressure** — if support deflection numbers look weak, the temptation will be to overstate what the assistant can do rather than report it plainly, which would undo Pillar 3 -e

## Part C — Product Glossary (Plain Language)

> [!abstract] Purpose of this document A plain-language glossary for anyone reading Rydex's product docs who isn't an engineer — stakeholders, new team members, reviewers. Every term here is one you'll hit in the [[1-PRD|PRD]], [Executive Summary (Part A above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-a--executive-summary), or [Vision & Strategy (Part B above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-b--vision--strategy) doc without it being spelled out in context.

> [!note] Not the same as the engineering glossary This is the **product** glossary — plain meaning, no implementation detail. The separate [[3.rydex-domain-and-architecture|domain glossary]] (Phase 0.2) is the **engineering** ubiquitous-language glossary — it defines exact entity states, the difference between "matched" and "accepted," and other precision needed for code and data modeling. If a term's _precise_ technical definition matters to you, go there instead.

## C1. People & Roles

|Term|Plain meaning|
|---|---|
|**Rider**|Someone who needs to get from point A to point B and requests a trip|
|**Driver**|Someone earning income by providing rides; goes "online" to become available for matching|
|**Sender**|The delivery variant of a Rider — someone who needs a package moved instead of themselves|
|**Ops / Support Agent**|Internal team member who resolves disputes, intervenes on problem trips, and handles escalations the AI assistant can't|
|**Platform Admin**|Internal team member who controls pricing rules, geographic zones, and driver onboarding — without needing an engineer to make those changes|

## C2. The Trip Lifecycle

|Term|Plain meaning|
|---|---|
|**Trip**|One end-to-end ride or delivery, from request to completion (or cancellation)|
|**Trip request**|The moment a rider sets a pickup/destination and asks to be matched with a driver|
|**Matching**|The process of connecting a specific rider to a specific available driver|
|**Trip status**|Where a trip currently sits in its lifecycle — requested, matched, driver en route, in progress, completed, or cancelled|
|**Cancellation window**|The period during which a rider or driver can cancel a trip without (or with reduced) penalty|
|**Trip history**|A rider's or driver's past completed/cancelled trips, with fare breakdowns and receipts|
|**Rating**|The two-way feedback riders and drivers give each other after a trip completes|

## C3. Matching & Dispatch

|Term|Plain meaning|
|---|---|
|**Nearest-driver matching**|The simplest matching approach — find the closest available driver and offer them the trip|
|**Batch matching**|When several riders request trips at nearly the same moment, matching all of them together as a group, in a way that's more efficient overall than matching one at a time|
|**Fairness-aware queueing**|Making sure drivers who have been waiting the longest get priority access to new ride offers, rather than always favoring whoever happens to be closest|
|**Match acceptance rate**|Of the matches the system offers, how many drivers actually accept — a signal of whether matches are genuinely good ones|

## C4. Pricing

|Term|Plain meaning|
|---|---|
|**Base fare**|The fare calculated from distance and time alone, before any surge|
|**Surge pricing**|A temporary price increase that kicks in when ride requests in an area outpace available drivers, and automatically fades once supply and demand even back out|
|**Zone**|A defined geographic area used to track local supply/demand and apply pricing rules specific to that area|
|**Driver payout**|What a driver actually earns from a trip, including any surge bonus|

## C5. Live Tracking & Real-Time Updates

|Term|Plain meaning|
|---|---|
|**Live tracking**|Seeing a driver's (or your own) position update continuously on a map, without needing to refresh|
|**Real-time / live update**|Information that updates the instant it changes, rather than waiting for the user to ask "anything new?"|
|**Driver presence**|Whether a driver is currently online (available), offline, or busy (on a trip)|
|**In-app notification**|A timely message like "Your driver has arrived" or "Your driver is 2 minutes away," delivered the moment it's true|

## C6. Trust & Safety

|Term|Plain meaning|
|---|---|
|**Fraud / anomaly detection**|Automatically spotting trip patterns that don't look normal — like a location that jumps impossibly far in a few seconds — and flagging them for a person to review|
|**Flagging**|Marking a trip as suspicious or disputed so a human can look at it; flagging never auto-cancels or auto-punishes on its own|
|**SOS / emergency flag**|A way for a rider or driver to raise an urgent alert during an active trip|
|**Dispute**|A complaint raised by either party about a completed trip (e.g., wrong fare, bad experience) that goes into a review workflow|

## C7. AI-Assisted Support

|Term|Plain meaning|
|---|---|
|**AI support assistant**|A help-chat feature that answers common questions automatically, using only the platform's own documented policies — not general internet knowledge|
|**Retrieval-based AI**|A way of answering questions where the AI looks up the actual relevant document first, then answers from it — instead of guessing from general training|
|**Smart escalation**|When the AI assistant can't confidently answer, it hands the conversation to a human agent along with everything already discussed, so the person doesn't have to repeat themselves|
|**Deflection rate**|The percentage of support questions the AI assistant resolves on its own, without needing a human agent|

## C8. Forecasting & Analytics

|Term|Plain meaning|
|---|---|
|**Demand forecasting**|Predicting how many ride requests are likely to come in soon, in a given area, based on past patterns — used to help drivers position themselves and to inform pricing|
|**Ops dashboard**|A live internal view of trips, matching performance, and overall system health|
|**Historical trip analytics**|Trends over time — ride volume, pricing behavior, cancellation rates — used for business decisions|

## C9. How the Platform Is Judged

|Term|Plain meaning|
|---|---|
|**Time-to-match**|How many seconds it takes, on average, for a rider to get connected with a driver after requesting a trip|
|**ETA accuracy**|How close the estimated arrival time is to the actual arrival time — the backbone of trust in live tracking|
|**Fraud flag precision**|Of the trips the fraud system flags, how many are actually problematic — a high number means the system is useful signal, not noise|
|**System uptime**|The percentage of time the platform is available and working correctly|
|**Load testing**|Deliberately simulating heavy real-world usage ahead of time, to find out — with evidence — what the system can actually handle, rather than guessing|
|**Observability**|The platform's ability to show, in real time, how healthy and fast it currently is — so problems are caught on a dashboard before a user has to report them|

## Part D — Personas

> [!abstract] Purpose of this document The [Executive Summary (Part A above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-a--executive-summary) lists _who_ Rydex serves in one table. This document goes one layer deeper for each persona: who they are, what they need, how they actually move through the product, and which features exist specifically because of them. Use this when designing a feature, writing acceptance criteria, or checking whether a decision serves the right person.

> [!note] Source Built from [[1-PRD|PRD]] §3 (Target Users & Personas) and §7 (User Journeys). Where the PRD only gives a one-line need (Sender, Ops Agent, Admin), the journey below is inferred from their stated need and the features tagged to them in PRD §6 — not invented from scratch.

## D1. Rider

**Who they are:** Someone who needs to get from point A to point B.

**Core need:** Fast matching, accurate ETA, transparent pricing, a sense of safety.

### Journey

1. Opens the app, sees nearby available drivers on a map
2. Enters a destination, requests a trip
3. Gets matched with a driver within seconds
4. Watches the driver approach in real time
5. Takes the trip, sees live status updates throughout
6. Trip ends, fare is charged automatically, rates the driver

### What this persona is most sensitive to

- **Latency** — a delay between requesting and getting matched is the single fastest way to lose this persona (per [Vision & Strategy (Part B above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-b--vision--strategy) Pillar 1)
- **Price surprises** — surge needs to be visible and explainable _before_ the trip is confirmed, not discovered on the receipt
- **"Where's my driver"** anxiety — live tracking exists almost entirely to neutralize this

### Features that exist primarily for this persona

|Feature|Epic|
|---|---|
|Request a trip, trip status tracking|B — Trip Lifecycle|
|Live driver location on map, trip notifications|C — Live Tracking|
|Base fare + surge transparency|E — Pricing|
|AI support assistant|G — AI-Assisted Support|
|SOS / emergency flag|F — Trust & Safety|

## D2. Driver

**Who they are:** Someone earning income by providing rides.

**Core need:** Fair access to ride offers, predictable earnings, low-friction navigation.

### Journey

1. Goes "online" — system starts tracking live location
2. Receives a ride offer with pickup distance and estimated fare
3. Accepts or declines (declining returns the offer to the matching pool)
4. Navigates to pickup, starts the trip, completes it
5. Sees earnings update, including any surge bonus

### What this persona is most sensitive to

- **Fairness of offers** — if the same well-positioned drivers always get offers while others wait indefinitely, trust in the platform erodes; this is the entire reason fairness-aware queueing exists, not just nearest-match
- **Idle time** — every minute without an offer is lost income; this is why batch matching aims to minimize _aggregate_ pickup distance, not just optimize each match individually
- **Earnings predictability** — surge bonuses need to be visible and explained, not a black box

### Features that exist primarily for this persona

|Feature|Epic|
|---|---|
|Driver onboarding, profile & preferences|A — Identity & Accounts|
|Driver presence (online/offline/busy)|C — Live Tracking|
|Fairness-aware queueing, batch matching|D — Matching & Dispatch|
|Driver payouts|E — Pricing & Payments|
|AI support assistant|G — AI-Assisted Support|

## D3. Sender _(delivery variant)_

**Who they are:** Someone who needs a package moved rather than themselves.

**Core need:** Reliable pickup/drop-off, live tracking, proof of delivery.

### Journey _(inferred from the Rider journey, adapted for delivery)_

1. Opens the app, sets pickup and drop-off locations for a package instead of a destination for themselves
2. Requests the trip; matched with a driver the same way a Rider would be
3. Watches the courier's progress in real time, same live-tracking mechanism as a Rider
4. Receives confirmation of delivery — likely a proof-of-delivery artifact (photo, signature, or code) rather than the Rider's "trip complete, rate your driver" flow

> [!note] Scope flag The PRD describes the Sender as a "delivery variant" of the Rider persona (§3) but does not give Sender a dedicated journey or feature row in §6/§7. The matching, tracking, and pricing mechanics are assumed shared with Rider. **Proof-of-delivery is implied by the need statement but has no corresponding feature row yet** — worth confirming whether this is intentionally deferred or a gap to raise in [[2.rydex-requirements-and-risk|traceability matrix]].

### What this persona is most sensitive to

- **Proof that the package actually arrived**, specifically — this is a different trust requirement than a Rider has, since the Sender is rarely present at drop-off to confirm it themselves
- Same live-tracking and ETA sensitivity as Rider

## D4. Ops / Support Agent

**Who they are:** Internal team member resolving issues.

**Core need:** Visibility into trips, ability to intervene, tools to resolve disputes quickly.

### Journey _(inferred from need statement + Epic F/G feature rows)_

1. A trip is flagged — either by the automated fraud/anomaly system, an SOS trigger, or a rider/driver dispute
2. Agent opens the flagged trip in an ops view, sees full trip context (location history, timing, fare breakdown)
3. If the AI support assistant already attempted to help the rider/driver and escalated, the agent receives the full prior conversation context — they don't make the person repeat themselves
4. Agent resolves the dispute or fraud flag, which may involve a refund, a warning, or closing the flag with no action

### What this persona is most sensitive to

- **Context loss** — the entire design intent of "smart escalation" (PRD §7, §10) is that this persona never starts from zero; if the AI hands off without full context, this persona's job gets harder, not easier
- **Signal vs. noise** — if the fraud system over-flags, this persona drowns in false positives; fraud flag precision (a KPI (Part A7 above)) exists specifically because this persona's time is finite

### Features that exist primarily for this persona

|Feature|Epic|
|---|---|
|Role-based access|A — Identity & Accounts|
|Dispute flagging, fraud/anomaly review queue|F — Trust & Safety|
|Smart escalation handoff|G — AI-Assisted Support|
|Ops dashboard|H — Analytics & Forecasting|

## D5. Platform Admin

**Who they are:** Manages pricing rules, zones, and driver onboarding.

**Core need:** Control over business rules without needing engineering help.

### Journey _(inferred from need statement + Epic I feature rows)_

1. Defines or adjusts a geographic zone (e.g., drawing a new surge-pricing boundary)
2. Sets or edits base pricing rules for a zone
3. Reviews pending driver applications — approves or rejects based on submitted documents
4. Monitors driver activity and suspends drivers if needed
5. Checks system health view to understand platform status in product terms, without needing to read engineering dashboards directly

### What this persona is most sensitive to

- **Not needing an engineer in the loop** — this is the explicit design goal (PRD §3); if a pricing change requires a deploy, this persona's need isn't met
- **Understanding system health without technical translation** — the "system health view" feature exists specifically to surface engineering observability data "in product terms" (PRD §6, Epic I)

### Features that exist primarily for this persona

|Feature|Epic|
|---|---|
|Zone & pricing rule management|I — Admin Console|
|Driver management (approve/suspend)|A & I|
|System health view|I — Admin Console|

## D6. Cross-Persona Patterns

A few things hold true across more than one persona, worth keeping in mind when designing any single feature:

- **Rider and Driver share the live-tracking and matching substrate** — almost every Epic C and D feature serves both sides of the same exchange simultaneously; a change that helps one without considering the other is incomplete by default
- **Ops Agent and Admin both need "engineering reality" translated into product terms** — neither persona should need engineering literacy to do their job, which is why both their primary features are explicitly described as surfacing existing technical data "in product terms" rather than as new technical capability
- **AI assistant serves both Rider and Driver identically** — the PRD doesn't differentiate the support experience by persona, which is itself a scope decision worth keeping consistent as the feature builds out -e

## Part F — Success Metrics (Detailed)

> [!abstract] Purpose of this document The [Executive Summary (Part A above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-a--executive-summary) lists Rydex's seven KPIs in one table. This document goes one layer deeper for each metric: what it measures, why it's the right proxy for that thing, what feature(s) it's downstream of, who's accountable for it moving, and the failure mode it exists to catch. The _exact numeric targets and measurement method_ (p95/p99, query, dashboard) belong to [[2.rydex-requirements-and-risk|SLO definitions]] (Phase 0.4) — this file defines **what** is measured and **why it matters**; that file defines **the number and how it's verified**.

> [!note] Source Metrics are taken directly from [[1-PRD|PRD]] §12 (Success Metrics / KPIs), cross-referenced against §11 (Non-Functional Requirements) where a direct pairing exists, and mapped to the features ([Feature Roadmap (Part E above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-e--feature-roadmap)) and personas ([Personas (Part D above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-d--personas)) that drive each one.

## F1. How These Metrics Relate to Each Other

Not all seven KPIs sit at the same level. Some are **outcome metrics** (what the business and users actually feel), and some are **system health metrics** (what makes the outcomes possible). Confusing the two leads to optimizing the wrong thing — chasing a health metric without checking it moved an outcome, or chasing an outcome without the health metric to explain _why_ it moved.

|Layer|Metrics|
|---|---|
|**Outcome** (felt by users, drives business value)|Time-to-match, Cancellation rate, ETA accuracy, AI support deflection rate|
|**Quality-of-mechanism** (is the underlying system doing its job well, not just fast)|Match acceptance rate, Fraud flag precision|
|**Platform health** (foundation everything else depends on)|System uptime / p95 latency|

> [!important] Why this layering matters A fast match (good Time-to-match) that drivers keep declining (poor Match acceptance rate) isn't actually a win — it's a sign the matching algorithm is producing technically-fast-but-bad matches. The two metrics have to be read together, which is exactly why [Vision & Strategy (Part B above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-b--vision--strategy) Pillar 1 frames matching quality as "fast _and_ fair," not fast alone.

## F2. Time-to-Match

**What it tells you:** How fast riders get connected to a driver.

**Why this metric, specifically:** Per [[1-PRD|PRD]] §11, slow matching is described as "the single fastest way to lose a rider" — this is the most consequence-sensitive metric on the list, which is why it gets the most explicit NFR language in the PRD ("match found within a few seconds under normal load").

**Driven by:** Nearest-driver matching (Alpha), Batch matching, Fairness-aware queueing (Beta 2) — see [Feature Roadmap (Part E above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-e--feature-roadmap) §3, §5.

**Primary persona affected:** Rider (directly); Driver (indirectly — slow matching means more idle time).

**Failure mode this catches:** A matching engine that's _correct_ but too slow to be felt as instant — the PRD is explicit that correctness without speed still loses the rider.

**Where the exact number lives:** [[2.rydex-requirements-and-risk|SLO definitions]] — this is the metric the Delivery Plan's own SLO table already starts to define (p95 < 2s, p99 < 3s, per [[3-End-to-End-Delivery-Plan|Delivery Plan]] §0.4), so that file should be treated as the authoritative target once written.

## F3. Match Acceptance Rate

**What it tells you:** Whether offered matches are actually _good_ matches — not just fast ones.

**Why this metric, specifically:** A high time-to-match with a low acceptance rate would mean the system is fast at making offers drivers don't want. This metric exists specifically to prevent Time-to-match from being gamed or misread in isolation (see §1 above).

**Driven by:** Nearest-driver matching, Batch matching, Fairness-aware queueing, Match performance benchmarking — see [Feature Roadmap (Part E above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-e--feature-roadmap) §3, §5.

**Primary persona affected:** Driver (this is fundamentally a measure of whether offers respect driver needs — pickup distance, fairness in queue position).

**Failure mode this catches:** A matching strategy that looks good on paper (e.g., minimizes some internal cost function) but produces offers drivers routinely decline in practice.

## F4. Cancellation Rate

**What it tells you:** A friction or dissatisfaction signal.

**Why this metric, specifically:** Cancellation is a lagging indicator that something upstream went wrong — a bad match, a price surprise, a slow pickup. It's the metric that aggregates failures from _other_ parts of the system rather than measuring one thing directly.

**Driven by:** Cancellation handling (Alpha) as the feature that _records_ it; but the rate itself is downstream of nearly every other feature — matching quality, pricing transparency, ETA accuracy.

**Primary persona affected:** Rider and Driver both — either side can cancel, and the PRD's cancellation handling feature explicitly captures a reason, which is what makes this metric diagnostic rather than just a count.

**Failure mode this catches:** Problems that don't show up cleanly in any single feature's own metrics but show up here as the common symptom — this is the metric to check first when something feels "off" without an obvious single cause.

## F5. ETA Accuracy

**What it tells you:** How trustworthy the live-tracking experience is.

**Why this metric, specifically:** Per [[1-PRD|PRD]] §11, "trust in live tracking depends entirely on" real-time update latency — ETA accuracy is the user-facing expression of that latency requirement. A technically-live map that shows a wrong ETA still breaks trust.

**Driven by:** Live driver location on map, Live trip status push (Beta 1) — see [Feature Roadmap (Part E above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-e--feature-roadmap) §4.

**Primary persona affected:** Rider.

**Failure mode this catches:** A real-time system that's _technically_ pushing updates on schedule but whose underlying ETA calculation (which depends on map/routing integration, per [[1-PRD|PRD]] §4 scope boundary) is wrong often enough to erode confidence even when the plumbing works.

## F6. Fraud Flag Precision

**What it tells you:** Whether the fraud system is useful signal or just noise.

**Why this metric, specifically:** Per [[1-PRD|PRD]] §10, fraud/anomaly detection is explicitly "not a guarantee of catching all fraud — a triage tool for human review." Precision (not recall) is the metric that matters most here, because the PRD's design intent (scoring, never auto-blocking) means a human — the Ops/Support Agent — pays the cost of every false positive directly, in wasted review time.

**Driven by:** Fraud/anomaly detection (Beta 3) — see [Feature Roadmap (Part E above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-e--feature-roadmap) §6.

**Primary persona affected:** Ops/Support Agent (directly, as the reviewer); Rider/Driver (indirectly, as the protected and occasionally-falsely-flagged party).

**Failure mode this catches:** A fraud model that flags aggressively to maximize catch rate but, in doing so, drowns the Ops Agent in false positives — exactly the risk named in [Personas (Part D above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-d--personas) §4 ("signal vs. noise").

## F7. AI Support Deflection Rate

**What it tells you:** How much support load the AI assistant genuinely absorbs.

**Why this metric, specifically:** This is the metric that keeps the AI honesty principle (PRD §10) accountable to a number rather than a claim. The PRD is explicit that the assistant "only knows what's in this platform's own knowledge base, and says so when it doesn't know" — deflection rate measures whether that honest, bounded design still produces real value, or whether being honest about its limits means it deflects too little to matter.

**Driven by:** AI support assistant, Smart escalation (Beta 3) — see [Feature Roadmap (Part E above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-e--feature-roadmap) §6.

**Primary persona affected:** Rider, Driver (experience faster resolution); Ops/Support Agent (experiences reduced load, assuming escalations arrive with full context per [Personas (Part D above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-d--personas) §4).

**Failure mode this catches:** Two opposite failure modes, both visible in this one number — a deflection rate near 0% means the assistant isn't useful; a suspiciously high rate paired with rising dispute/complaint volume would suggest it's answering confidently when it should be escalating (the exact overpromising risk flagged in [Vision & Strategy (Part B above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-b--vision--strategy) §6).

## F8. System Uptime / p95 Latency

**What it tells you:** Whether the platform holds up under real conditions.

**Why this metric, specifically:** This is the foundation metric — per [[1-PRD|PRD]] §11, the explicit framing is "graceful degradation rather than full outages," and per §14 Risks, all scale claims must be "backed by real load tests, not estimates." This is the metric that turns every other claim in this document from assertion into evidence, which is the entire premise of [Vision & Strategy (Part B above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-b--vision--strategy) Pillar 4.

**Driven by:** Not any single feature — this is a property of the whole platform, proven specifically at the Release Candidate milestone (load testing, dashboards) per [Feature Roadmap (Part E above)](https://claude.ai/chat/931cc535-a10d-4882-adff-50ece26a8b63#part-e--feature-roadmap) §7.

**Primary persona affected:** All — this is the metric whose failure is felt by every persona simultaneously and immediately.

**Failure mode this catches:** A system that works in every individual feature demo but has never been proven to hold up under realistic concurrent load — the gap between "it works" and "it's been shown to work," which is precisely the distinction the Delivery Plan's Phase 7 (Evidence Pass) exists to close.

**Where the exact number lives:** [[2.rydex-requirements-and-risk|SLO definitions]] — availability target (e.g., 99.5% over rolling 30 days) and the load-test report itself, per [[3-End-to-End-Delivery-Plan|Delivery Plan]] §7.2.

A consolidated view, useful when deciding which metric to check after a given feature ships:

|Metric|Primary feature(s)|Primary persona|
|---|---|---|
|Time-to-match|Nearest-driver / Batch matching, Fairness queueing|Rider|
|Match acceptance rate|Matching strategies, Benchmarking|Driver|
|Cancellation rate|Cancellation handling (symptom of many features)|Rider, Driver|
|ETA accuracy|Live tracking, status push|Rider|

|AI support deflection rate|AI support assistant, Smart escalation|Rider, Driver, Ops Agent|
